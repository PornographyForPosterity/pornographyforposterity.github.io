About the Pornography for Posterity Project - Mon, Feb 10, 2020

What Pornography for Posterity is, why it exists, and what it is not.

What this project is

Pornography for Posterity (PFP) is an independent, volunteer-run effort to catalog and preserve the historical and biographical record of commercial pornographic film and photography — performers, studios, releases, and the surrounding context that made them significant. It exists because pornography is one of the most widely consumed and least documented media categories in modern history. Newspapers do not run obituaries for its performers. Libraries do not accession its releases. Studios fold, tapes degrade, websites disappear, and the paper trail that would let a future researcher understand this industry — who worked in it, under what names, for which studios, in what era — thins out a little more every year.

PFP treats that record as worth keeping, the same way a film archive, a newspaper morgue, or a museum’s ephemera collection is worth keeping: not as endorsement of the material, but as a bet that future historians, sociologists, and curious people will want to know what actually happened, and that nobody else is reliably writing it down.

What this project is not

This site does not host, sell, or distribute pornographic films or images. Entries here are catalog metadata — names, aliases, studio and release information, dates, categories, and citations to other public records — not the underlying media itself. If you arrived here looking for adult content to view, this is the wrong site; if you arrived here looking for the documentation of adult content, you’re in the right place.

Scope

The project’s initial focus is the pre-digital and early-digital era of commercial pornography — roughly the period before “tube sites” and user-generated platforms made the volume of new content effectively uncountable. That earlier era is the part of the record most at risk of being lost entirely: it was produced by a comparatively small number of studios and performers, distributed on physical media, and documented (if at all) in trade publications and box covers that are themselves disappearing. The modern era is not out of scope forever, but cataloging it well requires different priorities than cataloging a finite, closing chapter of history, so it’s being deliberately deferred.

See Methodology for how entries are built and sourced, and Scope & Ethics for what does and doesn’t get included.
