### Why the pre-digital era first

Commercial pornographic film has a documentable production history stretching back decades before the internet — a relatively small number of studios, shot on film and later analog video, distributed through theaters, mail order, and video rental. That era has a natural boundary: it ended, and the record of it is fixed in size even as pieces of it are actively being lost. Studios closed and their archives scattered or were destroyed. Video releases degrade or were never transferred off obsolete formats. Performers from this period are aging or have died with no obituary anywhere recording what they actually did professionally.

That combination — a finite, closing record that is actively decaying — is what makes it the right place to start. It's the part of the historical record where documentation work has the highest ratio of urgency to volume.

### Why not everything, immediately

The explosion of user-generated and platform-hosted content from roughly the mid-2000s onward changed the scale of the industry by orders of magnitude. Cataloging that era with the same per-entry approach used for the studio era doesn't scale, and applying studio-era standards of "who is a notable performer or release" doesn't cleanly translate to a landscape of independent creators and self-published content. That era deserves its own methodology, not an afterthought bolted onto this one.

### What comes next

As the project matures, expect scope to expand outward from the studio era — first toward the early digital/DVD era, which shares most of the studio era's structure, and only later toward genuinely user-generated content, once there's a methodology suited to it. None of that is scheduled; it happens as volunteer capacity allows. See [Contribute](/contribute/) if you'd like to help it happen sooner.
